## Introduction

Brake system violations remain one of the leading causes of vehicle **Out-of-Service (OOS)** orders during roadside inspections in the United States. Under **49 CFR Part 393**, commercial motor vehicles must meet strict brake performance and component standards. When those standards are not met, enforcement action is often immediate.

Unlike minor documentation errors, brake deficiencies are treated as high-risk safety violations because they directly affect stopping distance and crash severity.

For a broader overview of vehicle compliance requirements, see our [Vehicle Inspection and Maintenance Requirements Guide](/dot-vehicle-inspection-maintenance-requirements/).

---

## What Inspectors Evaluate

During Level I or Level II inspections, officers commonly check:

- Pushrod stroke limits (brake adjustment)
- Inoperative brakes
- Air system integrity (leaks, low pressure warnings)
- Brake lining thickness
- Contaminated or damaged components

If a defined percentage of brakes are out of adjustment or inoperative, the vehicle may be placed Out of Service under CVSA OOS criteria.

Air brake system deficiencies, including significant air loss or failure to maintain pressure, are also enforcement-sensitive issues.

---

## Enforcement Impact

A brake-related OOS order typically results in:

- Immediate shutdown until repairs are completed  
- Roadside repair or towing costs  
- Delivery delays and load disruption  
- CSA Vehicle Maintenance BASIC score impact  

Repeated brake violations increase inspection frequency and insurance scrutiny. In many cases, enforcement action occurs not because of catastrophic failure, but because of incremental neglect or incomplete inspection routines.

Brake violations are frequently discovered alongside issues such as [Tire Defects and Out-of-Service Criteria](/tire-defects-out-of-service-criteria/) and missing inspection documentation.

---

## Why Brake Violations Occur

Common contributing factors include:

- Inadequate pre-trip inspections  
- Failure to measure pushrod stroke  
- Lack of air system leak testing  
- Inconsistent maintenance documentation  

Owner-operators operating under tight delivery schedules may unintentionally deprioritize brake measurement checks, especially when no obvious performance issue is felt during driving.

## Preventive Control Measures

To reduce brake-related enforcement risk:

1. Conduct structured pre-trip brake inspections.  
2. Measure pushrod stroke against manufacturer limits.  
3. Perform periodic air system leak-down testing.  
4. Maintain documented inspection records.  
5. Address minor deficiencies before roadside exposure.

Driver-side compliance issues such as log accuracy are addressed separately in our [Hours-of-Service Rules Guide](/hours-of-service-rules/).

Brake compliance should be treated as a risk management discipline rather than a reactive repair issue.
