When a commercial motor vehicle is involved in an accident, the clock starts immediately — not for the insurance adjuster, not for the repair estimate, but for your regulatory compliance window. The decisions made and documented in the first four hours post-incident determine whether your carrier emerges with a defensible record or becomes another data point in [FMCSA's enforcement statistics](https://www.fmcsa.dot.gov/safety/data-and-statistics). This is not conjecture. It is the operational reality encoded in federal regulation and confirmed by enforcement patterns across thousands of post-accident investigations.

---

## What the Regulation Actually Requires: 49 CFR 390.15 and the Accident Register

Under [49 CFR 390.15](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-49/subtitle-B/chapter-III/subchapter-B/part-390/section-390.15), every motor carrier must maintain an accident register for three years from the date of each recordable accident. The register must include the date and nature of the accident, city or town, driver name, number of injuries, number of fatalities, and whether hazardous materials were released. This is not optional documentation — it is a mandatory record with a defined retention window, and failure to maintain it constitutes a violation that surfaces directly during compliance reviews.

The broader recordkeeping obligations that feed into post-accident compliance are outlined in detail in our [DOT recordkeeping and document retention guide](https://blog.thetruckercodex.com/dot-recordkeeping-document-retention/), which covers retention schedules across all major document categories. Cross-reference that resource before your next audit.

### What Constitutes a "Recordable Accident" Under Federal Standards

Not every incident triggers the full federal accident protocol, but carriers routinely misclassify events and either over-report or — more dangerously from an enforcement standpoint — under-report. Under 49 CFR 390.5, a recordable accident involves:

- A fatality
- A bodily injury requiring immediate medical treatment away from the scene
- Disabling damage to any motor vehicle requiring tow-away

If any of these conditions are met, the full post-accident chain of obligations activates. Misclassifying a tow-away as a non-reportable incident is one of the most frequently cited errors during compliance audits.

## The Post Accident DOT Testing Protocol: Timelines That Cannot Slip

The post accident DOT testing protocol is governed primarily by 49 CFR Part 382, Subpart C, and it operates on hard deadlines — not targets, not estimates. Two windows control the validity of the test result.

### Alcohol Testing: The 8-Hour Cutoff

A post-accident alcohol test must be administered as soon as practicable, and if it cannot be completed within eight hours of the accident, the carrier must cease attempts and document the reason. If the test is not administered within two hours, the carrier must document why. Every hour of delay without documentation is potential evidence of non-compliance during an investigation.

### Drug Testing: The 32-Hour Cutoff

For controlled substances, the testing window extends to 32 hours post-accident. However, "32 hours" is not an invitation to delay. Carriers that routinely test near the outer boundary of this window — without documented cause for the delay — attract heightened scrutiny during audits. The test must use a USDOT-regulated collection site and a certified laboratory.

### Driver Removal From Service

A driver who cannot be tested within the applicable windows is subject to removal from safety-sensitive functions until a test is completed. Documenting that removal, and the reasoning behind it, is as important as the test itself. Failure to remove creates compounding liability, particularly when the incident is reviewed as part of a pattern during a CSA intervention.

## The Four-Hour Action Sequence: What Must Happen and When

The first four hours are not reserved for paperwork alone — they are when the compliance record is actually built or destroyed. Here is the operational sequence every carrier should be executing:

- **Immediately (0–15 minutes):** Notify the safety department or designated compliance officer. Confirm whether the incident meets the recordable accident threshold.
- **Within 1 hour:** Initiate drug and alcohol testing referral. Contact a collection site or a consortium/third-party administrator (C/TPA) if one is contracted.
- **Within 2 hours:** Document all attempts to arrange testing. If testing has not begun, the reason must be recorded in writing.
- **Within 4 hours:** Secure the driver's hours of service records, ELD data, and any dashcam footage. These records are time-sensitive and subject to spoliation claims if not preserved. Review the [complete list of compliance documents](https://blog.thetruckercodex.com/dot-compliance-documents-the-complete-list-truck-drivers-must-maintain/) your driver should have on hand for law enforcement and subsequent investigation.
- **Within 24 hours:** Begin completing the accident register entry under 390.15. Do not wait for insurance finalization to populate this record.

## Enforcement Patterns: Where Carriers Get Caught

FMCSA enforcement data identifies a consistent cluster of post-accident failures during compliance reviews. Carriers that accumulate violations in this area rarely make a single catastrophic error — they fail across multiple small process points that individually look minor but collectively signal systemic non-compliance.

### Common Violation Codes Associated With Post-Accident Non-Compliance

The violations most frequently cited in this context include:

- **392.80 / 392.82:** Texting or handheld device use (often surfaced during accident reconstruction, adding to pre-accident driver behavior record)
- **395.8:** Hours of service record violations discovered when ELD data is pulled post-accident
- **382.303:** Failure to conduct post-accident testing within required timeframes
- **390.15(b):** Failure to maintain a complete accident register

Each of these feeds directly into the BASIC scores that determine intervention thresholds. ## Small Carrier Exposure: Disproportionate Risk

Small carriers — typically those operating fewer than six power units — face outsized exposure in post-accident scenarios. They often lack a dedicated safety officer, have no C/TPA relationship established before an incident, and have not pre-identified collection sites in their operating territory. When an accident occurs, these operational gaps become compliance gaps in real time.

This mirrors the structural vulnerabilities that lead to audit failures across other compliance areas. Our analysis of [why small fleets fail DOT audits](https://blog.thetruckercodex.com/why-small-fleets-fail-dot-audits-the-6-most-common-dqf-defic/) identifies pre-established process infrastructure as the primary differentiator between carriers that survive audits and those that receive unsatisfactory ratings.

## Building a Defensible Post-Accident File

A defensible post-accident compliance record contains: completed accident register entry, documented testing timeline with collection site confirmation or documented attempt failure, driver's ELD or paper log from the relevant shift, law enforcement report number and jurisdiction, and any internal incident notification records with timestamps.

That file must be retained for three years minimum under 390.15 — longer if litigation is pending or reasonably anticipated.
